Gambling Statistics 2021

Despite being a centuries-old pastime that has generated billions of dollars for the economy over the years, gambling is an activity that is still frowned upon – particularly when it comes to US legislation.

The industry is defined by its high-class casino venues, online gambling apps and websites, and licensed sportsbooks. As COVID-19 and all its many variants continue to plague the globe, commercial gaming operations have been heavily impacted in the states, leading to multiple business closures, inconceivable losses in brick-and-mortar gaming revenue, and an overall decline in consumers.

Michael Cohen

Updated: 6 October 2021

The Impact of COVID-19 on the Gambling Industry

The pandemic alone has had a significant effect on the gambling industry since 2020, resulting in a 31.3% decline in revenue. The industry smashed its record of $43.65 billion in 2019, whereas, in 2020, commercial gaming revenue barely topped $30 billion thanks to a heavy contraction in the market.

The following study breaks down the performance of the commercial gaming industry in the US from 2020 to 2021, outlining key financial metrics and data for the 29 states currently permitted to operate commercial gaming. The key findings below are taken from (and credited to) the American Gaming Association’s State of the States report for 2021.

  • $30.0 billion generated in commercial gaming revenue in 2020 - the first market contraction since 2014 and the lowest total since 2003.

  • 45,600 lost business days for commercial casinos due to pandemic-related closures, or approximately 27% of potential operating days.

  • $6.7 billion paid in gaming taxes to state and local governments, with billions more supported in sales, income, and other taxes.

YOY Commercial Gaming Revenue in the US

Compared to 2019, all 25 states where physical casino gaming is legal reported significantly lower revenues for every quarter in 2020. The scale of the declines in each state varied and was traced back to the length of time casinos needed to be legally closed, along with their performance after reopening following new, strict Covid-19 business guidelines.

According to the AGA, New Mexico took the biggest brunt when casinos were mandated to close in March 2020 and remained with their shutters down for the rest of the year. North of the country, South Dakota experienced the least impact after it was permitted to reopen just seven weeks after the first lockdown.

Even though admissions and visits remained depressed for the regional markets compared to pre-pandemic levels, entries in October showed no visible declines for places like Las Vegas. The gambling capital continues to see a strong rebound and welcomed over 3.4 million visitors in October 2021, with the spending levels for consumers also surpassing pre-pandemic levels. According to available data for the five key states, average casino wins per visitor increased between 18.5% and 44.9% compared to October 2019.

Fortunately, 2021 has also seen commercial gaming revenue bounce back substantially for other states. Compared to a stagnant 10-month period in 2019, year-to-date through to October saw a 19.8% increase, totaling USD 43.68 billion in commercial gaming revenue in 2021, with only New Mexico remaining at a flat -0.7%. 11 out of 25 commercial gaming jurisdictions all surpassed revenues for 2019, and the following states: Iowa, Colorado, South Dakota, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, and Michigan all beat their annual record for 2020.

In November, six different states gave the thumbs up for gaming expansion referendums, indicating that Americans still had a soft spot for the gambling industry in 2020.

And while physical casinos became overwhelmed by the pandemic, online gaming and sports betting saw immense growth. This led to the legalization of seven brand new markets that boosted sports betting revenues in the states by 69%, equating to almost $1.6 billion. Across the 4 operational iGaming markets, 2020 saw them collectively generate revenues of $1.6 billion, which was triple the total revenues for 2019.

“The gaming industry faced enormous challenges in 2020 – and we also saw significant changes, as player demographics shifted and emerging verticals saw strong growth. From sharp revenue declines to booming legal sports betting activity and overwhelming voter enthusiasm behind gaming, this year’s report reflects both the highs and lows of the past year.” - AGA President and CEO Bill Miller.
